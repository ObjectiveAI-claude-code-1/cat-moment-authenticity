Task 1 — Assess Temporal Specificity: Look at the photograph and determine how precisely it is locked to a single instant in time. Ask whether this image could only have existed at this exact moment — whether a half-second earlier or later would have produced a fundamentally different picture. Look for evidence of peak actions caught at their apex: the height of a jump, the widest point of a yawn, the instant of contact between paw and object. Look equally for unstable transitions between states — the liminal instant between sleep and waking, between stillness and pounce, between attention and dismissal — moments that cannot be held or posed because they are inherently fleeting. An image where the cat is simply sitting in a stable, settled posture that it might hold for minutes at a time should score low, because the shutter could have fired at any point in that window and captured essentially the same scene. An image that feels torn from a specific fraction of a second — where the body is caught in motion, the expression is mid-change, or the arrangement of the scene is visibly temporary — should score high.

Task 2 — Assess Individuality and Particularity: Examine the photograph and determine how strongly it conveys the identity of a specific, individual cat rather than a generic representative of the species. Look at posture, expression, and the physical idiosyncrasies of the animal — the particular way it arranges its body, tilts its head, or holds its paws. Then look at the relationship between the cat and its environment: does the setting feel claimed, inhabited, and specific to this creature's life, or is it interchangeable and anonymous? A photograph where the cat has wedged itself into a space that fits its particular body, or is interacting with a familiar object in a way that suggests routine and habit, or is occupying its environment with the ease of long ownership should score high. A photograph where the cat could be swapped for any other cat without changing the meaning of the image — where neither the animal's personality nor its relationship to the space is visible — should score low. The goal is to distinguish portraits of individuals from generic records of the species.

Task 3 — Assess Narrative Presence: Evaluate the photograph for the degree to which it implies a story unfolding across time rather than presenting a flat, resolved, static fact. Ask whether the image creates the sensation of walking into the middle of a scene — whether the viewer feels that something has just happened, is actively happening, or is about to happen. Look for body language that suggests intention, reaction, or anticipation: ears rotated toward a sound, weight shifted forward before a leap, eyes locked on something outside the frame, a paw extended mid-reach. Look for tension between elements in the scene — a cat and a hand approaching each other, a cat poised at the edge of a surface, a cat watching something move. Even in stillness, narrative presence can exist when the scene implies waiting, watching, or the passage of time. An image that feels complete and resolved — where nothing is about to happen and nothing has just occurred, where the viewer looks, confirms a cat exists, and moves on — should score low. An image that invites the viewer to imagine the moments just before and just after the frame, that feels like one scene pulled from a longer, ongoing story, should score high.
